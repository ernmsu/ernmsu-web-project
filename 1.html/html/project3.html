A circle is one of the most fundamental shapes in geometry, defined as the set of all points in a plane that are at a fixed distance (the radius) from a central point. It has no corners or edges, giving it perfect symmetry in every direction. Circles appear frequently in nature and human design—from ripples in water to wheels and planetary orbits. Their properties are deeply studied in mathematics, especially in relation to concepts like circumference, area, and the constant π, which describes the ratio between a circle’s circumference and its diameter.

A tetrahedron is a three-dimensional solid composed of four triangular faces, making it the simplest type of polyhedron. Each face is a triangle, and all faces meet at four vertices. A regular tetrahedron has all faces as equilateral triangles, with equal edge lengths and angles, giving it a high degree of symmetry. This shape is important in fields such as chemistry and crystallography, where molecular structures often resemble tetrahedral arrangements. Despite its simplicity, it has fascinating geometric and spatial properties.

A square is a two-dimensional shape with four equal sides and four right angles. It is a special type of rectangle and also a regular quadrilateral, meaning all its sides and angles are equal. Squares are commonly used in architecture, design, and tiling because of their ability to fit together without gaps. The symmetry of a square makes it easy to analyze mathematically, and its area and perimeter are straightforward to calculate. Its balanced proportions give it both practical and aesthetic appeal.
